Federal Legal Reference Library

The U.S. Constitution (Indexed)
Articles of the Constitution

Article I - III (Branches of Gov)

Establishes the Legislative (Congress), Executive (Presidency), and Judicial (Federal Courts) branches, defining their separate powers and checks on one another.

Article IV - VII (States & Ratification)

Outlines the duties states have to each other, the amendment process (V), establishes the Constitution as the Supreme Law of the Land (VI), and covers ratification (VII).

The Bill of Rights

First Amendment

Protects freedom of religion, speech, press, assembly, and the right to petition the government.

Second Amendment

Protects the right of the people to keep and bear arms.

Fourth Amendment

Protects against unreasonable searches and seizures. Requires warrants to be judicially sanctioned and supported by probable cause.

"The right of the people to be secure in their persons, houses, papers, and effects... shall not be violated."

Fifth Amendment

Guarantees due process, protects against self-incrimination ("pleading the fifth"), prohibits double jeopardy, and requires a grand jury for capital crimes.

Sixth Amendment

Guarantees the right to a speedy and public trial by an impartial jury, the right to confront accusers, and the right to legal counsel.

Eighth Amendment

Prohibits excessive bail, excessive fines, and cruel and unusual punishments.

Further Amendments

Fourteenth Amendment

Grants citizenship to all born or naturalized in the US. The Equal Protection Clause and Due Process Clause apply constitutional rights to the state/local level.

Federal Case Law Precedents
Search, Seizure & Vehicle Stops

Terry v. Ohio (1968)

Allows police to briefly detain a person based on reasonable suspicion of involvement in criminal activity. Officers may conduct a limited "frisk" of outer clothing for weapons if they reasonably believe the suspect is armed and dangerous.

Carroll v. United States (1925)

Established the "Motor Vehicle Exception." Police may search a vehicle without a warrant if they have probable cause to believe it contains contraband or evidence, due to the vehicle's mobility.

Interrogation & Counsel

Miranda v. Arizona (1966)

Criminal suspects in police custody must be informed of their constitutional right to an attorney and against self-incrimination before police questioning.

Use of Force & Medical Aid

Graham v. Connor (1989)

Established the "objective reasonableness" standard. Force must be judged from the perspective of a reasonable officer on the scene.

Tennessee v. Garner (1985)

Lethal force may not be used to prevent the escape of a fleeing suspect unless the officer has probable cause to believe the suspect poses a significant threat of death or serious physical injury to the officer or others.

City of Revere v. Mass. General Hospital (1983)

The Due Process Clause of the 14th Amendment requires the responsible government agency to provide medical care to persons who have been injured while being apprehended by the police.

Discovery & Municipal Liability

Brady v. Maryland (1963)

The prosecution must turn over all exculpatory evidence (evidence that might exonerate the defendant) to the defense. Withholding such evidence violates due process.

Monell v. Dept. of Social Services (1978)

Allows a municipality (City/Police Dept) to be sued under 42 U.S.C. § 1983 if an unconstitutional action implements or executes a policy statement, ordinance, regulation, or a tolerated "custom" of misconduct.
